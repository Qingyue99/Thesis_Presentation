Lifestyle Geography Of Food Environments

Boston as a Case Study

By Qingyue Li

Lifestyle in Urban Scenario

Urban experiences are diverse and dynamic, and can be expressed from many different perspectives. One perspective to express the urban lifestyle is from public spaces.


In my thesis, I focus on how to see lifestyle through the lens of food environments.


Why Food environment is a good lens to see Urban Lifestyle?

Maslow's basic human needs theory gave me a lot of inspiration. Food and the food-related environment covered all three major stages of his model,from basic needs, to psychological needs, to self-fulfillment needs.

The food related environment can be classified by its different functions.

Zoom in: Back Bay as a Microscale Food Environment in Boston

Observation is how this project began. From typical weekdays to weekends, I document the scenes of people interacting with food-related environments, like restaurants, supermarkets and public dining spaces.


I started my observation from where I live, Back Bay. I find that the Prudential Center surroundings is a microscale public space that meets different people’s needs at different times of the day.

Zoom Out to Food Environments in Boston

After exploring near my home, I started to expand my observation to greater area in Boston.


I observe that food-related public space in different neighborhood have distinct difference to each other. I saw the white-collar going out of skyscrapers buying food from food truck as the lunch break; I knew many of my friends enjoy going to Allston, at 7 pm at weekends, to devour some cheap but authentic Korean and Japanese food; etc.


The dynamic of Boston lies in its colorful food environment, and this food environment shapes its residents' lifestyle.

Define Lifestyle Geography

In this thesis, I define the term of Lifestyle Geography, to see how one’s lifestyle influences one’s geographic location, and how geographies reflect lifestyles. Lifestyle Geography describes the relationship between behaviors and physical geography.


In this study, I will focus on four factors of lifestyle geography, including relationship status, where one lives, where one works, and one’s age. When the combination of these 4 factors change, lifestyle geography also changes.

Two Strategies to Understand Lifestyle Geography From Data Perspectives

I mainly use two strategies in this research and analysis investigating process: quantitative research, including an Online Questionnaire and Geospatial Data, and qualitative research including Observational Research.

Working Neighborhoods vs. Living Neighborhoods: Food Amenities Distribution

From the survey, the Top 3 living neighborhoods are Back Bay, Mission Hill, and Brighton.
Top 3 working neighborhoods are Back Bay, Downtown and Fenway-Kenmore.


Based on food amenities geolocation data I gained from Google API, I categorize the data into the three types of food amenities, and then map it to the top neighborhoods in each category.




This cluster chart compares the number of different kind of food amenities in all these five neighborhoods. For each kind of food amenity, from left to right shows corresponding numbers. Separated by the middle red bar, Back Bay, the left side of each cluster shows numbers in living neighborhoods. Working neighborhoods are on the right.


The working neighborhoods generally have more cafes, convenient stores, shopping mall, and all kinds of restaurants (from cheap to-go restaurants to highend fancy restaurants). This difference in number helps working neighborhoods embrace a faster pace of lifestyle. This quantitative anlaysis is aligned with my previous observations.


In the meantime, living neighborhoods have the edge on some livable aspects, such as liquor stores and supermarkets. These advantages would appear more clearly when we analyze the tolerable travel time to grocery in my later discussion.


Now it's time for the Second Story

In my questionaire, people were asked "What are your top 3 food amenities choices?" With the answers from this question as well as some demographic information, I drew the following two cluster charts. The first one focus on showing how age influences people's choices. This is inspired by Professor Dietmar when we did the thesis defense rehearsal. The second one is for comparing the selections by different marital status. I have to point out that age and marital status have some positive correlation; we sometimes need to combine the these two results together to obtain an objective views and conclusions.
1. Here's the first example, bars. With the age becomes older and people's tendency to get married, they go to the bars more and more often. This is indeed a little bit hard to tell which is more powerful factor. Similar findings can also be observed when we compare the cafes, We can see a drop at age 24 to 28 or in a relationship.
2. However, when I inspect the different tendency of age and marital status, some interesting key points can be subtracted. First, let's talk about fine dining. We can see from the age chart that once people have enough financial power, I assume, they go to the fine dining at a pretty stable frequency. On the other hand, if we look at the marital status chart, we can see a obvious increase in fine dining frequency if people are in relationships or married. Married people do know romance better!
3. With similar analysis process, some results can also be found in the casual dining and supermarkets categories. Older people go to casual sit-down restaurants more frequently; Married people go to supermarkets less often.
4. In some categories, increasing age and getting married have similar effects; as people grow old or get married, they take less and less fast food. Meal kits suffer a devastated indifference no matter in what kind of groups.

The Third Story is about Public Transportation Enhance People’s Food-Related Lifestyle

Boston is one of the city in United States has a relatively well-developed public transportation system. In Boston, the subway is the most popular public transportation mode across all demographics.
Thus, It maybe interesting to see how MBTA subway stations enhance food related lifestyle: eat socially where food is the necessary focal point,eat socially where food is not necessary or the focal point, buy unprepared foods for cooking at home by what amounts of food amenities nearby.


Boston has 145 stations.
Brighton has 6 subway stations within its boundary. I draw a buffer zone based on each center of the station location in order to see how many food amenities people can access near each subway station. In Brighton, Warren Street is the most accessible station to food amenities, while Boston College is the least accessible station to food amenities.


Contribution & Conclusion

1. Collect, clean and integrate food-related data in Boston.
Lack of datasets is always the first problem when anybody studies Boston. Boston professionals in urban visualization sometimes have to study New York City to test their method only because of insufficient Boston open datasets. In this thesis, I collected data on food-related amenities using Google API, gathered residents' habits by questionnaires, obtained geographical boundaries, MBTA data, open space data, and environmental audit data from open source.
2. Develop a general method to study the coexistence and coevolution of space and people's habit.
In this thesis, I explore the method to organically utilize both objective geo-space information and subjective personal information. Under this scheme, some interesting findings are obtained when I analyze my data across different neighborhoods, ages, marital statuses, and transportations. Related details are already well-demonstrated in the discussion above. I also want to point out that this method could be used much more universally than this case study; it could be applied to different cities and focus on different topics besides food.
3. Design delicate and informative graphics to help readers better observe the problems and further gain insights.
Information design is crucial in my thesis, or the mixture of my data from all kinds of domains could easily confuse readers. I utilized map-based graphics, cluster chart graphics, cartoon-based graphics, etc. All these graphics were specifically designed to organizedly provide adequate information to the readers. This practice and experience can also be adapted to other projects.
